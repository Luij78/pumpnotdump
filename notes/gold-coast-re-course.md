## Notes

### Subsection 1: Disciplinary Authority of the Commission

**Commission Powers:** [F.S. 475.25(1)]
- May **deny** application for licensure
- May **discipline** licensees for violation of F.S. 475 or any rule under its authority

**Disciplinary Actions for Guilty Licensee:**
- Issued a **reprimand**
- **Administrative fine up to $5,000 per offense**
- Placed on **probation**
- License **suspended for up to 10 years**
- License **permanently revoked**

**Legal Authority:**
- **F.S. 455** "The Regulation of Professions and Occupations Act" — provides authority for investigations and hearings
- **F.S. 120** "Administrative Procedures Act" — establishes hearing procedures
- Commission can consider **mitigating OR aggravating** circumstances

---

### Subsection 2: Range of Penalties

**Purpose of disciplinary guidelines:** To give notice to licensees of the range of penalties that would normally be imposed for each count during a formal or informal hearing.

**Order of Penalties (lowest to highest):**
1. Reprimand
2. Fine
3. Probation
4. Suspension
5. Revocation or Denial

**Key Points:**
- Disciplinary guidelines are based on a **single violation**
- Guidelines don't preclude discipline via stipulation, settlement agreement, or letter of guidance
- **Maximum penalties:** Revocation or denial of license
- Commission **cannot deny payment of a commission** to a licensee

**Minor Violations:**
- May be dealt with by issuing a **citation or notice of noncompliance**
- Investigators for the Department can only issue citations/notices for minor offenses **specified by the Commission**

---

### Subsection 3: Property Management Violations

**Overview:** The Commission deals with an increasing number of property management violation cases. With sales associates, these typically involve licensees acting as investors OR beyond scope.

**Licensees who act as investors or beyond the scope of a sales associate:**

**(Slide 1) Licensee acting as an investor:**
- If acting as investor, licensee must ensure **ALL PARTIES ARE AWARE** they are **NOT acting as a real estate licensee**
- **Example Case:** Individual acting as investor gave out business cards/stationary indicating they were a licensee → Commission ruled they WERE acting as a licensee, not investor

**• Licensee acting in the capacity of an investor:**
- A licensee CAN act as a private investor
- **HOWEVER:** If the licensee leads the public to believe they're dealing in a real estate brokerage transaction, AND the other party is damaged/harmed → that party may collect from the **Real Estate Recovery Fund**
- Result: **Automatic suspension** to the licensee

**• Licensee acting beyond the scope of a sales associate:**
- A sales associate may **ONLY receive compensation from their employing broker**
- Cannot receive compensation from any other source regarding real estate transactions

**Example Case:**
- Sales associate's broker doesn't handle property management
- Sales associate sets up their OWN company to handle property management
- **Result:** Found GUILTY of operating as a broker while licensed as a sales associate

# Chapter 3: Evaluating and Pricing Property

## Section 1: Appraisal Regulation and Appraisals vs. CMAs

### Appraisals vs. CMAs (Slide 1 of 3)

**Appraising** = the process of developing an opinion of value of real property.

**Key characteristics of an appraisal:**
- Appraiser conducts an **independent, impartial, and objective analysis**
- Based on research and data pertaining to the value of **specified real property**
- **Appraisal fees** are based on the **time, effort, and expense** involved — NOT on the value of the property

**Situations requiring an appraisal:**
- Lenders determining if collateral value is adequate for a loan
- **Federal income tax claims**
- **Federal estate taxes**
- **Real estate tax assessments**
- **Eminent domain**
- **Investment planning**
- **Insurance claims**
- And many other circumstances

### Appraisal Regulation (Slide 2 of 2)

**Federally Related Transaction:**
- Any real estate related financial transaction that a **Federal Financial Institution Regulatory Agency (FFIRA)** has either **contracted for or regulates and requires** the services of an **appraiser**
- Appraisal reports involving a federally related transaction must be prepared by a **state certified appraiser**

**USPAP Compliance:**
- All appraisals used in a federally related transaction must be prepared in **compliance with USPAP**
- By appraisers who are registered, licensed, or certified under **Part II of F.S. 475**
- The **Florida Real Estate Appraisal Board (FREAB)** administers Part II of F.S. 475

## Section 2: Valuation

### Topic 1: Types of Value (Slide 1 of 5)

**Valuation** = the process of determining the value of real property as of a given date.

**Value** (USPAP definition) = an opinion of the worth of a property at a given time in accordance with a specific definition of value. It is the monetary relationship between properties and those who buy, sell, or use those properties.

**In appraisal practice, value must ALWAYS be qualified:**
- Market value
- Liquidation value
- Investment value

**Types of value — an appraisal or CMA will reflect the type of value sought by the client.**

**Common types of value:**
- **Assessed value** = value assigned by the property appraiser for **ad valorem tax** purposes
- **Insured value** = face amount that a casualty or hazard insurance policy will pay if property is judged unusable

### Topic 1: Types of Value (Slide 2 of 5)

- **Investment value** = value of a **particular property to a particular investor**
  - Potential purchasers of income-producing properties commonly request investment value appraisals
  - Investment value = highest price an investor will pay / lowest price seller will accept
  - Investment value is to a **specific individual**, while **market value** is in a typical transaction to a typical buyer
- **Liquidation value** = amount remaining after all assets of a business have been sold in a hurried (but not forced) sale and all liabilities paid
  - It is the value of a **failing business** that is not expected to continue
  - Can also estimate the **minimum value** of a profitable business
  - Typically estimated for **financial institutions considering foreclosure** on a property

### Topic 1: Types of Value (Slide 3 of 5)

- **Market value** = the **most often estimated value** in RE valuation
  - The amount that SHOULD be paid for a property
  - **NOT necessarily** the amount asked or actually paid
  - Market value can be **higher or lower** than the cost or price
- **USPAP definition of Market Value:** "A type of value, stated as an opinion, that presumes the transfer of a property (i.e., a right of ownership or a bundle of such rights), as of a certain date, under specific conditions set forth in the definition of the term identified by the appraiser as applicable in an appraisal."

### Topic 1: Types of Value (Slide 4 of 5)

- **Market value (USPAP cont'd):** Forming an opinion of market value is the purpose of many RE appraisal assignments, particularly when the client's intended use includes more than one intended user.
- **Three categories of conditions** in market value definitions:
  1. The **relationship, knowledge, and motivation** of the parties (seller and buyer)
  2. The **terms of sale** (cash, cash equivalent, or other terms)
  3. The **conditions of sale** (exposure in a competitive market for a reasonable time prior to sale)

### Topic 1: Types of Value (Slide 5 of 5)

- **Use value** (aka **value-in-use**) = the value the property holds for the owner
  - Factors that contribute to use value:
    - **Income** — property can produce income through leases (investors pay for the income flow)
    - **Appreciation** — property generally increases in value over time
    - **Use** — specific use (residential, agricultural, commercial, recreational) determines value and benefits
    - **Tax benefits** — capital gains, tax losses, tax deferrals, and depreciation

### Topic 2: Price vs. Value (Slide 1 of 1)

- **Price** = the amount **actually paid** in a RE transaction
  - NOT necessarily the asking amount or amount offered
  - May NOT represent the actual market value
  - Can be **more than or less than** market value
  - It IS the amount buyer is willing to pay AND seller is willing to accept
- **Examples:**
  - Forced sale (job transfer) → property sells **below** market value
  - Includes personal property or buyer has special circumstances → sells **above** market value

### Topic 3: Appraisal Principles (Slide 1 of 1)

**Principle of Anticipation:**
- The **value of a property today is the sum of its future benefits**
- Buyer considers the benefits during their period of ownership → forms basis for the decision to buy and at what price
- Value today is measured in terms of **future benefits**
- Particularly visible in **income-producing RE** — present dollars paid in exchange for future dollars
- **Example:** Buyer hears property down the street may become a shopping mall — benefit or disadvantage? Depends on buyer's viewpoint

**Principle of Change:**
- **Circumstances can cause changes to occur in the market**, which may affect RE value
- An **appraisal is made as of a specific date** to account for market forces at that point in time
- **Types of changes that affect RE value:**
  - Volume of inventory
  - Rate of sale or time on market
  - Forces of supply and demand
  - Interest rates
  - Availability of financing
  - Unemployment rates

**Principle of Competition:**
- Sellers compete with other sellers, buyers compete with other buyers
- Focuses on the **effect of changes in supply and demand**
- **Factors affecting supply and demand:**
  - Number of properties available in an area
  - Property prices
  - Number of prospective buyers
  - Price buyers are willing to pay
- **Example:** 1 home in a desirable neighborhood = more value than if 4 homes were for sale there. Multiple fast-food restaurants on same street attract more buyers than one alone.

**Principle of Conformity:**
- **Value is sustained when a property is in conformity with other properties in the same area**
- Conformity = size, architectural style, and other features
- **Example:** 3bed/1bath in a neighborhood where all homes have 2 baths → owner should consider adding a 2nd bathroom to conform

**Principle of Contribution:**
- **Value of a component = the amount it increases the total property value** (NOT what it cost)
- The amount the property would decrease by its absence
- **Contribution ≠ Cost** — it's what the MARKET recognizes as the change in value
- **Examples:**
  - Remodeled kitchen costs $25k-$75k but adds $50k to value → **contribution = $50,000**
  - Pool costs $30k to install but adds only $15k to value → **contribution = $15,000**

**Principle of Progression:**
- A **lower-priced property in an area of more expensive properties** will **increase** (progress) in value toward the higher-priced homes
- This principle tends to **create price conformity** within an area
- **Example:** Smallest house on the block increases in value because the other homes are worth more
- 🏠 = "Worst house on the best block" — value goes UP

**Principle of Regression:**
- A **higher-priced property in an area of lower-priced properties** will **decrease** (regress) in value toward the cheaper homes
- Like progression, tends to **create price conformity** within an area
- **Example:** Largest house on the block decreases in value if other homes are much lower in value
- 🏠 = "Best house on the worst block" — value goes DOWN

**Principle of Substitution:**
- **No one would pay more for a property than the amount necessary** to acquire an acceptable substitute
- This is the **basis for ALL mathematical methods** used by appraisers to estimate value
- **Example:** If several similar homes (size, quality, amenities) are for sale in a neighborhood, a buyer will usually NOT purchase the one with the highest price

### Topic 4: Appraisal Purpose and Intended Use (Slide 1 of 1)

**Purpose vs. Intended Use — they are DISTINCT:**

- **Purpose** = to estimate some type of defined value
  - Relates to the work the appraiser was retained to perform
  - Most appraisals are performed to estimate **market value**

- **Intended Use** = how the client will USE the appraisal (previously called "function" in USPAP)
  - Client may use it to decide whether to sell/buy and at what price
  - Lender may use it to decide whether to make a loan using that property as security

### Appraisals vs. CMAs (Slide 2 of 3)

**RE Licensees and Appraisals:**
- Real estate licensees **may perform an appraisal** under RE license law IF the valuation is **not to be used in a federally related transaction**
- However, brokers and sales associates who offer opinions of value usually do **NOT** perform an appraisal — they prepare a **CMA** instead

**Comparative Market Analysis (CMA):**
- A **value estimate based on recent sales of similar properties** in the same neighborhood
- Utilizes many of the appraisal concepts
- ⚠️ **A CMA may NOT be referred to as an appraisal**

### Step 2: Scope of Work (Slide 1 of 1) ✅

- The appraiser must determine the **type and extent of research and analyses** necessary to develop credible assignment results
- Scope of work is an **ongoing process** — new information discovered during the assignment might cause the appraiser to reconsider the scope
- **Key takeaway:** Scope of work isn't set in stone, it can change as you learn more

### Step 3: Data Collection and Analysis (Slide 1 of 3)

- The primary activity of the appraisal process is the **selection, gathering, and analysis of data**
- Data is collected and assembled for use throughout the appraisal process
- As data is collected, it is selected and analyzed for **accuracy and relevance (reliability)**
- **Two types of data:**
  1. **General data** — concerns the region, neighborhood, economy
  2. **Specific data** — information about the subject property and potential comparable properties

### Step 3: Data Collection and Analysis (Slide 2 of 3) — Highest and Best Use Analysis

- Every property **has a single use which produces the greatest income and return**
- Property will have its highest value when used for that purpose
- Highest and best use analysis indicates whether existing improvements **contribute to the value**, or if they should be removed to permit a more profitable use
- Also determines if an **alternative use would provide a greater return** on the investment

### Step 3: Data Collection and Analysis (Slide 3 of 3) — Highest and Best Use (cont.)

- For this analysis, the property's use must be one or all of:
  - **Legally permissible**
  - **Physically possible**
  - **Financially feasible**
  - **Maximally productive**
- **Example:** Old office building in downtown area undergoing residential redevelopment → best use might be conversion to high-end condominium units


## Chapter 3, Section 3: The Appraisal Process

### Step 3: Data Collection and Analysis — Highest and Best Use Analysis
For this analysis, the property's use must be one or all of the following:
- **Legally permissible**
- **Physically possible**
- **Financially feasible**
- **Maximally productive**

**Example:** A property with an old office building may not be in its highest and best use if located in a downtown area undergoing residential redevelopment. Its best use might be conversion to high-end condominium units.

### Step 4: Apply the Three Approaches to Value
Three mathematical methods appraisers use to estimate value:
1. **The cost-depreciation approach**
2. **The income approach**
3. **The comparable sales approach**

All three are employed to the extent applicable, unless the assignment does not require one or more. Each yields slightly different results, which must then be **reconciled**. Each approach is discussed later in this chapter.

### Step 5: Reconciliation of Value Indications and Final Opinion of Value
After the three approaches have been applied and each has resulted in a value estimate, the three estimates are compared. The appraiser's confidence in the data and the appropriateness of the approaches to the assignment are weighed. Greater weight is given to the approach that the appraiser feels best reflects the value of the subject, and then a final value is estimated.

**KEY: Weighing the evidence and arriving at a final value is based on the appraiser's knowledge, experience, and training. It is NOT accomplished by averaging the values or using a mathematical process.**

### Step 6: Report the Defined Value
The objective of the appraisal is to answer the client's original question regarding value of the rights in the subject property. Once final value is estimated, the appraiser prepares a report for the client.

Report types:
- **Form reports** — Used in millions of appraisals each year. Most primary lenders and secondary market require them. Standardizes information, facilitates underwriting. **This is the reporting preference for most residential appraisals.**
- **Narrative reports** — Very comprehensive. Provide client with reasoning and conclusions in a detailed report (50 to 300+ pages). Length varies depending on nature of assignment and client requirements.
- **Oral reports** — Generally given only in connection with **court testimony**. Appraisers who provide court testimony must follow same procedures as written reports and must maintain files supporting their conclusions and testimony.

## Chapter 3, Section 4: Approaches to Estimating Value

### Introduction
When performing a formal appraisal, appraisers typically use three approaches:
1. **Cost-depreciation approach**
2. **Income approach**
3. **Comparable sales approach**

All three are used if the assignment, available data, and client requirements do not limit their application. Each yields slightly different results and tends to give a more reliable estimate for a particular property type. The differences must be reconciled into a final value conclusion.

### Cost-Depreciation Approach
Formula: Cost of reproducing/replacing building - Depreciation + Land value = Property value

Based on the **principle of substitution**: no one would pay more for an existing property than the cost to purchase land and have comparable improvements constructed on it (assuming no unusual time delay).

Value estimated using either **replacement cost** or **reproduction cost**.

Two types of cost:
1. **Replacement cost** — Estimated cost at current prices to construct a comparable building with equal utility using modern materials, design, and features. NOT necessarily same materials — some may no longer be available, substitution may be necessary.
2. **Reproduction cost** — Estimated cost at current prices to construct an exact duplicate or replica using the same materials, design, and layout. **Preferred in appraisals of historic properties.**

## Chapter 3, Section 5: The CMA (Comparative Market Analysis)

### Step 1: Gather Property Information
- Collect data on the subject property and comparable properties
- Do NOT include crime rates or ethnic makeup (Fair Housing Act violation)

### Step 2: Select Comparable Properties
- Best comps: sold within **6 months**, same neighborhood, similar features
- Avoid distressed sales (short sales, foreclosures) — not arm's length
- Pending sales show **listing price**, NOT sales price — unreliable for value
- Sales over **2 years old** are too outdated

### Step 3: Adjust Comparables
- **ALWAYS adjust the COMPARABLE, never the subject**
- **Comp lacks a feature** → ADD value to comp
- **Comp has an extra feature** → SUBTRACT value from comp
- **Matched pair technique** — Compare two similar properties with one difference to isolate that feature's value
- **Square footage technique** — Most commonly used adjustment method

### Step 4: Determine Realistic Selling Price
- Most reliable comp = the one with the **fewest adjustments**
- Market sets VALUE, seller sets ASKING PRICE
- Give seller a **RANGE**, not an exact figure
- CMA is an opinion of value, NOT an appraisal

## Chapter 3, Section 6: Short Sales, REOs, and the CMA

### Introduction
- Abundance of distressed properties creates unique CMA challenges
- **Always prefer non-distressed comps** when possible
- In heavy distressed markets, those sales become market reality and pull values down
- Seller must compete in the market that exists, including distressed properties
- Example: If distressed sales at $150/sqft and listings at $150-$160/sqft, seller can't expect $250/sqft

### Broker Price Opinion (BPO)
- **BPO** = estimate of value for a **bank-owned (REO) property**
- Also called an **Automated Valuation Model (AVM)** — submitted by computer
- **REO** = Real Estate Owned — property obtained through foreclosure, deed in lieu, or short sale
- **Who can prepare a BPO in Florida:** Anyone with an active **broker, sales associate, or appraisal license**
- Preparer is entitled to **compensation**

### BPO Report Contents
- Neighborhood analysis of comparable properties
- Local and regional market information
- **Factors affecting price:** values of similar properties, sales trends, amount of repair needed
- **BPO hierarchy:** Less thorough than an appraisal, but more analysis than a basic CMA

### Outsourcers
- **Outsourcer** = an **asset management company** that handles the entire REO process from assignment to broker to sale
- Many lenders turn REO assets over to outsourcers rather than handling them directly

### Why BPOs Matter for Your Career
- Buyer's market → more distressed owners → more REOs → more need for BPO preparers
- BPO pay isn't huge, but builds **relationships with lenders** → they trust you to list their REO portfolio → new income stream

# Chapter 4: Prospecting

## Section 1: Prospecting

### Introduction (Slide 1 of 2)
- **Prospecting** = the process of looking for new business; this process NEVER stops
- Foundation of your RE business — buyers, sellers, closings, commissions all stem from prospecting
- Spend a **majority of your time** on prospecting
- Develop a truly aggressive and consistent prospecting plan
- **Leverage** social sites to communicate, share, and grow sphere of influence exponentially

### Introduction (Slide 2 of 2)
**Three keys to prospecting success:**
1. **Number of contacts** — volume matters
2. **Consistency of contacts** — regular outreach, not just when you need business
3. **System for tracking contacts** — CRM or method to follow up

### Your Database (Slide 1 of 2)
- **Information management** is key to all three prospecting success factors
- Better data management = more success
- Contact management software/mobile apps = quick access from any device
- More automated prospecting = more people reached consistently, less time/effort
- **CRM** tracks contacts, reminds when to call/email/text, automates some tasks
- Start with **five categories**, create a separate database for each

### Your Database (Slide 2 of 2)
**Five prospecting categories (database groups):**
1. **Sphere of Influence** — people you already know
2. **Expired Listings** — listings that didn't sell with previous agent
3. **For Sale By Owner (FSBO)** — owners selling without an agent
4. **Geographic Farm** — specific neighborhood you target consistently
5. **Past Clients** — repeat business and referrals

### Sphere of Influence (expanded)
- Start here FIRST — compile a list of ALL people you know and have interacted with
- Includes: friends, family, hair stylist, mechanic, doctor, lawyer, accountant, server, bartender, civic orgs, clubs, sporting events, school acquaintances — literally everyone
- Tip: input **10 people per day** until full list is entered
- List grows over time — add new people as you meet them

### Sphere of Influence — Prospecting Technique
- Tell people you're always looking for new business
- Ask permission to add them to your prospecting list
- Frame it as: "I'm growing my business and could use your help"
- Most people **love to help someone they admire and trust**
- Key: make sure your SOI regards you with trust and admiration

### Expired Listings (Slide 1 of 1)
- Properties placed on market but **failed to sell**
- Advantage: you already know asking price, commission, and that they want to sell
- Sellers are **extremely motivated** — property sat for months
- **Check MLS daily** for expired listings in your area
- Enter into your expired listings database
- Can **automate through MLS home page**
