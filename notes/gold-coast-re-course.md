## Notes

### Subsection 1: Disciplinary Authority of the Commission

**Commission Powers:** [F.S. 475.25(1)]
- May **deny** application for licensure
- May **discipline** licensees for violation of F.S. 475 or any rule under its authority

**Disciplinary Actions for Guilty Licensee:**
- Issued a **reprimand**
- **Administrative fine up to $5,000 per offense**
- Placed on **probation**
- License **suspended for up to 10 years**
- License **permanently revoked**

**Legal Authority:**
- **F.S. 455** "The Regulation of Professions and Occupations Act" — provides authority for investigations and hearings
- **F.S. 120** "Administrative Procedures Act" — establishes hearing procedures
- Commission can consider **mitigating OR aggravating** circumstances

---

### Subsection 2: Range of Penalties

**Purpose of disciplinary guidelines:** To give notice to licensees of the range of penalties that would normally be imposed for each count during a formal or informal hearing.

**Order of Penalties (lowest to highest):**
1. Reprimand
2. Fine
3. Probation
4. Suspension
5. Revocation or Denial

**Key Points:**
- Disciplinary guidelines are based on a **single violation**
- Guidelines don't preclude discipline via stipulation, settlement agreement, or letter of guidance
- **Maximum penalties:** Revocation or denial of license
- Commission **cannot deny payment of a commission** to a licensee

**Minor Violations:**
- May be dealt with by issuing a **citation or notice of noncompliance**
- Investigators for the Department can only issue citations/notices for minor offenses **specified by the Commission**

---

### Subsection 3: Property Management Violations

**Overview:** The Commission deals with an increasing number of property management violation cases. With sales associates, these typically involve licensees acting as investors OR beyond scope.

**Licensees who act as investors or beyond the scope of a sales associate:**

**(Slide 1) Licensee acting as an investor:**
- If acting as investor, licensee must ensure **ALL PARTIES ARE AWARE** they are **NOT acting as a real estate licensee**
- **Example Case:** Individual acting as investor gave out business cards/stationary indicating they were a licensee → Commission ruled they WERE acting as a licensee, not investor

**• Licensee acting in the capacity of an investor:**
- A licensee CAN act as a private investor
- **HOWEVER:** If the licensee leads the public to believe they're dealing in a real estate brokerage transaction, AND the other party is damaged/harmed → that party may collect from the **Real Estate Recovery Fund**
- Result: **Automatic suspension** to the licensee

**• Licensee acting beyond the scope of a sales associate:**
- A sales associate may **ONLY receive compensation from their employing broker**
- Cannot receive compensation from any other source regarding real estate transactions

**Example Case:**
- Sales associate's broker doesn't handle property management
- Sales associate sets up their OWN company to handle property management
- **Result:** Found GUILTY of operating as a broker while licensed as a sales associate

# Chapter 3: Evaluating and Pricing Property

## Section 1: Appraisal Regulation and Appraisals vs. CMAs

### Appraisals vs. CMAs (Slide 1 of 3)

**Appraising** = the process of developing an opinion of value of real property.

**Key characteristics of an appraisal:**
- Appraiser conducts an **independent, impartial, and objective analysis**
- Based on research and data pertaining to the value of **specified real property**
- **Appraisal fees** are based on the **time, effort, and expense** involved — NOT on the value of the property

**Situations requiring an appraisal:**
- Lenders determining if collateral value is adequate for a loan
- **Federal income tax claims**
- **Federal estate taxes**
- **Real estate tax assessments**
- **Eminent domain**
- **Investment planning**
- **Insurance claims**
- And many other circumstances

### Appraisal Regulation (Slide 2 of 2)

**Federally Related Transaction:**
- Any real estate related financial transaction that a **Federal Financial Institution Regulatory Agency (FFIRA)** has either **contracted for or regulates and requires** the services of an **appraiser**
- Appraisal reports involving a federally related transaction must be prepared by a **state certified appraiser**

**USPAP Compliance:**
- All appraisals used in a federally related transaction must be prepared in **compliance with USPAP**
- By appraisers who are registered, licensed, or certified under **Part II of F.S. 475**
- The **Florida Real Estate Appraisal Board (FREAB)** administers Part II of F.S. 475

## Section 2: Valuation

### Topic 1: Types of Value (Slide 1 of 5)

**Valuation** = the process of determining the value of real property as of a given date.

**Value** (USPAP definition) = an opinion of the worth of a property at a given time in accordance with a specific definition of value. It is the monetary relationship between properties and those who buy, sell, or use those properties.

**In appraisal practice, value must ALWAYS be qualified:**
- Market value
- Liquidation value
- Investment value

**Types of value — an appraisal or CMA will reflect the type of value sought by the client.**

**Common types of value:**
- **Assessed value** = value assigned by the property appraiser for **ad valorem tax** purposes
- **Insured value** = face amount that a casualty or hazard insurance policy will pay if property is judged unusable

### Topic 1: Types of Value (Slide 2 of 5)

- **Investment value** = value of a **particular property to a particular investor**
  - Potential purchasers of income-producing properties commonly request investment value appraisals
  - Investment value = highest price an investor will pay / lowest price seller will accept
  - Investment value is to a **specific individual**, while **market value** is in a typical transaction to a typical buyer
- **Liquidation value** = amount remaining after all assets of a business have been sold in a hurried (but not forced) sale and all liabilities paid
  - It is the value of a **failing business** that is not expected to continue
  - Can also estimate the **minimum value** of a profitable business
  - Typically estimated for **financial institutions considering foreclosure** on a property

### Topic 1: Types of Value (Slide 3 of 5)

- **Market value** = the **most often estimated value** in RE valuation
  - The amount that SHOULD be paid for a property
  - **NOT necessarily** the amount asked or actually paid
  - Market value can be **higher or lower** than the cost or price
- **USPAP definition of Market Value:** "A type of value, stated as an opinion, that presumes the transfer of a property (i.e., a right of ownership or a bundle of such rights), as of a certain date, under specific conditions set forth in the definition of the term identified by the appraiser as applicable in an appraisal."

### Topic 1: Types of Value (Slide 4 of 5)

- **Market value (USPAP cont'd):** Forming an opinion of market value is the purpose of many RE appraisal assignments, particularly when the client's intended use includes more than one intended user.
- **Three categories of conditions** in market value definitions:
  1. The **relationship, knowledge, and motivation** of the parties (seller and buyer)
  2. The **terms of sale** (cash, cash equivalent, or other terms)
  3. The **conditions of sale** (exposure in a competitive market for a reasonable time prior to sale)

### Topic 1: Types of Value (Slide 5 of 5)

- **Use value** (aka **value-in-use**) = the value the property holds for the owner
  - Factors that contribute to use value:
    - **Income** — property can produce income through leases (investors pay for the income flow)
    - **Appreciation** — property generally increases in value over time
    - **Use** — specific use (residential, agricultural, commercial, recreational) determines value and benefits
    - **Tax benefits** — capital gains, tax losses, tax deferrals, and depreciation

### Topic 2: Price vs. Value (Slide 1 of 1)

- **Price** = the amount **actually paid** in a RE transaction
  - NOT necessarily the asking amount or amount offered
  - May NOT represent the actual market value
  - Can be **more than or less than** market value
  - It IS the amount buyer is willing to pay AND seller is willing to accept
- **Examples:**
  - Forced sale (job transfer) → property sells **below** market value
  - Includes personal property or buyer has special circumstances → sells **above** market value

### Topic 3: Appraisal Principles (Slide 1 of 1)

**Principle of Anticipation:**
- The **value of a property today is the sum of its future benefits**
- Buyer considers the benefits during their period of ownership → forms basis for the decision to buy and at what price
- Value today is measured in terms of **future benefits**
- Particularly visible in **income-producing RE** — present dollars paid in exchange for future dollars
- **Example:** Buyer hears property down the street may become a shopping mall — benefit or disadvantage? Depends on buyer's viewpoint

**Principle of Change:**
- **Circumstances can cause changes to occur in the market**, which may affect RE value
- An **appraisal is made as of a specific date** to account for market forces at that point in time
- **Types of changes that affect RE value:**
  - Volume of inventory
  - Rate of sale or time on market
  - Forces of supply and demand
  - Interest rates
  - Availability of financing
  - Unemployment rates

**Principle of Competition:**
- Sellers compete with other sellers, buyers compete with other buyers
- Focuses on the **effect of changes in supply and demand**
- **Factors affecting supply and demand:**
  - Number of properties available in an area
  - Property prices
  - Number of prospective buyers
  - Price buyers are willing to pay
- **Example:** 1 home in a desirable neighborhood = more value than if 4 homes were for sale there. Multiple fast-food restaurants on same street attract more buyers than one alone.

**Principle of Conformity:**
- **Value is sustained when a property is in conformity with other properties in the same area**
- Conformity = size, architectural style, and other features
- **Example:** 3bed/1bath in a neighborhood where all homes have 2 baths → owner should consider adding a 2nd bathroom to conform

**Principle of Contribution:**
- **Value of a component = the amount it increases the total property value** (NOT what it cost)
- The amount the property would decrease by its absence
- **Contribution ≠ Cost** — it's what the MARKET recognizes as the change in value
- **Examples:**
  - Remodeled kitchen costs $25k-$75k but adds $50k to value → **contribution = $50,000**
  - Pool costs $30k to install but adds only $15k to value → **contribution = $15,000**

**Principle of Progression:**
- A **lower-priced property in an area of more expensive properties** will **increase** (progress) in value toward the higher-priced homes
- This principle tends to **create price conformity** within an area
- **Example:** Smallest house on the block increases in value because the other homes are worth more
- 🏠 = "Worst house on the best block" — value goes UP

**Principle of Regression:**
- A **higher-priced property in an area of lower-priced properties** will **decrease** (regress) in value toward the cheaper homes
- Like progression, tends to **create price conformity** within an area
- **Example:** Largest house on the block decreases in value if other homes are much lower in value
- 🏠 = "Best house on the worst block" — value goes DOWN

**Principle of Substitution:**
- **No one would pay more for a property than the amount necessary** to acquire an acceptable substitute
- This is the **basis for ALL mathematical methods** used by appraisers to estimate value
- **Example:** If several similar homes (size, quality, amenities) are for sale in a neighborhood, a buyer will usually NOT purchase the one with the highest price

### Topic 4: Appraisal Purpose and Intended Use (Slide 1 of 1)

**Purpose vs. Intended Use — they are DISTINCT:**

- **Purpose** = to estimate some type of defined value
  - Relates to the work the appraiser was retained to perform
  - Most appraisals are performed to estimate **market value**

- **Intended Use** = how the client will USE the appraisal (previously called "function" in USPAP)
  - Client may use it to decide whether to sell/buy and at what price
  - Lender may use it to decide whether to make a loan using that property as security

### Appraisals vs. CMAs (Slide 2 of 3)

**RE Licensees and Appraisals:**
- Real estate licensees **may perform an appraisal** under RE license law IF the valuation is **not to be used in a federally related transaction**
- However, brokers and sales associates who offer opinions of value usually do **NOT** perform an appraisal — they prepare a **CMA** instead

**Comparative Market Analysis (CMA):**
- A **value estimate based on recent sales of similar properties** in the same neighborhood
- Utilizes many of the appraisal concepts
- ⚠️ **A CMA may NOT be referred to as an appraisal**

### Step 2: Scope of Work (Slide 1 of 1) ✅

- The appraiser must determine the **type and extent of research and analyses** necessary to develop credible assignment results
- Scope of work is an **ongoing process** — new information discovered during the assignment might cause the appraiser to reconsider the scope
- **Key takeaway:** Scope of work isn't set in stone, it can change as you learn more

### Step 3: Data Collection and Analysis (Slide 1 of 3)

- The primary activity of the appraisal process is the **selection, gathering, and analysis of data**
- Data is collected and assembled for use throughout the appraisal process
- As data is collected, it is selected and analyzed for **accuracy and relevance (reliability)**
- **Two types of data:**
  1. **General data** — concerns the region, neighborhood, economy
  2. **Specific data** — information about the subject property and potential comparable properties

### Step 3: Data Collection and Analysis (Slide 2 of 3) — Highest and Best Use Analysis

- Every property **has a single use which produces the greatest income and return**
- Property will have its highest value when used for that purpose
- Highest and best use analysis indicates whether existing improvements **contribute to the value**, or if they should be removed to permit a more profitable use
- Also determines if an **alternative use would provide a greater return** on the investment

### Step 3: Data Collection and Analysis (Slide 3 of 3) — Highest and Best Use (cont.)

- For this analysis, the property's use must be one or all of:
  - **Legally permissible**
  - **Physically possible**
  - **Financially feasible**
  - **Maximally productive**
- **Example:** Old office building in downtown area undergoing residential redevelopment → best use might be conversion to high-end condominium units


## Chapter 3, Section 3: The Appraisal Process

### Step 3: Data Collection and Analysis — Highest and Best Use Analysis
For this analysis, the property's use must be one or all of the following:
- **Legally permissible**
- **Physically possible**
- **Financially feasible**
- **Maximally productive**

**Example:** A property with an old office building may not be in its highest and best use if located in a downtown area undergoing residential redevelopment. Its best use might be conversion to high-end condominium units.

### Step 4: Apply the Three Approaches to Value
Three mathematical methods appraisers use to estimate value:
1. **The cost-depreciation approach**
2. **The income approach**
3. **The comparable sales approach**

All three are employed to the extent applicable, unless the assignment does not require one or more. Each yields slightly different results, which must then be **reconciled**. Each approach is discussed later in this chapter.

### Step 5: Reconciliation of Value Indications and Final Opinion of Value
After the three approaches have been applied and each has resulted in a value estimate, the three estimates are compared. The appraiser's confidence in the data and the appropriateness of the approaches to the assignment are weighed. Greater weight is given to the approach that the appraiser feels best reflects the value of the subject, and then a final value is estimated.

**KEY: Weighing the evidence and arriving at a final value is based on the appraiser's knowledge, experience, and training. It is NOT accomplished by averaging the values or using a mathematical process.**

### Step 6: Report the Defined Value
The objective of the appraisal is to answer the client's original question regarding value of the rights in the subject property. Once final value is estimated, the appraiser prepares a report for the client.

Report types:
- **Form reports** — Used in millions of appraisals each year. Most primary lenders and secondary market require them. Standardizes information, facilitates underwriting. **This is the reporting preference for most residential appraisals.**
- **Narrative reports** — Very comprehensive. Provide client with reasoning and conclusions in a detailed report (50 to 300+ pages). Length varies depending on nature of assignment and client requirements.
- **Oral reports** — Generally given only in connection with **court testimony**. Appraisers who provide court testimony must follow same procedures as written reports and must maintain files supporting their conclusions and testimony.

## Chapter 3, Section 4: Approaches to Estimating Value

### Introduction
When performing a formal appraisal, appraisers typically use three approaches:
1. **Cost-depreciation approach**
2. **Income approach**
3. **Comparable sales approach**

All three are used if the assignment, available data, and client requirements do not limit their application. Each yields slightly different results and tends to give a more reliable estimate for a particular property type. The differences must be reconciled into a final value conclusion.

### Cost-Depreciation Approach
Formula: Cost of reproducing/replacing building - Depreciation + Land value = Property value

Based on the **principle of substitution**: no one would pay more for an existing property than the cost to purchase land and have comparable improvements constructed on it (assuming no unusual time delay).

Value estimated using either **replacement cost** or **reproduction cost**.

Two types of cost:
1. **Replacement cost** — Estimated cost at current prices to construct a comparable building with equal utility using modern materials, design, and features. NOT necessarily same materials — some may no longer be available, substitution may be necessary.
2. **Reproduction cost** — Estimated cost at current prices to construct an exact duplicate or replica using the same materials, design, and layout. **Preferred in appraisals of historic properties.**

## Chapter 3, Section 5: The CMA (Comparative Market Analysis)

### Step 1: Gather Property Information
- Collect data on the subject property and comparable properties
- Do NOT include crime rates or ethnic makeup (Fair Housing Act violation)

### Step 2: Select Comparable Properties
- Best comps: sold within **6 months**, same neighborhood, similar features
- Avoid distressed sales (short sales, foreclosures) — not arm's length
- Pending sales show **listing price**, NOT sales price — unreliable for value
- Sales over **2 years old** are too outdated

### Step 3: Adjust Comparables
- **ALWAYS adjust the COMPARABLE, never the subject**
- **Comp lacks a feature** → ADD value to comp
- **Comp has an extra feature** → SUBTRACT value from comp
- **Matched pair technique** — Compare two similar properties with one difference to isolate that feature's value
- **Square footage technique** — Most commonly used adjustment method

### Step 4: Determine Realistic Selling Price
- Most reliable comp = the one with the **fewest adjustments**
- Market sets VALUE, seller sets ASKING PRICE
- Give seller a **RANGE**, not an exact figure
- CMA is an opinion of value, NOT an appraisal

## Chapter 3, Section 6: Short Sales, REOs, and the CMA

### Introduction
- Abundance of distressed properties creates unique CMA challenges
- **Always prefer non-distressed comps** when possible
- In heavy distressed markets, those sales become market reality and pull values down
- Seller must compete in the market that exists, including distressed properties
- Example: If distressed sales at $150/sqft and listings at $150-$160/sqft, seller can't expect $250/sqft

### Broker Price Opinion (BPO)
- **BPO** = estimate of value for a **bank-owned (REO) property**
- Also called an **Automated Valuation Model (AVM)** — submitted by computer
- **REO** = Real Estate Owned — property obtained through foreclosure, deed in lieu, or short sale
- **Who can prepare a BPO in Florida:** Anyone with an active **broker, sales associate, or appraisal license**
- Preparer is entitled to **compensation**

### BPO Report Contents
- Neighborhood analysis of comparable properties
- Local and regional market information
- **Factors affecting price:** values of similar properties, sales trends, amount of repair needed
- **BPO hierarchy:** Less thorough than an appraisal, but more analysis than a basic CMA

### Outsourcers
- **Outsourcer** = an **asset management company** that handles the entire REO process from assignment to broker to sale
- Many lenders turn REO assets over to outsourcers rather than handling them directly

### Why BPOs Matter for Your Career
- Buyer's market → more distressed owners → more REOs → more need for BPO preparers
- BPO pay isn't huge, but builds **relationships with lenders** → they trust you to list their REO portfolio → new income stream

# Chapter 4: Prospecting

## Section 1: Prospecting

### Introduction (Slide 1 of 2)
- **Prospecting** = the process of looking for new business; this process NEVER stops
- Foundation of your RE business — buyers, sellers, closings, commissions all stem from prospecting
- Spend a **majority of your time** on prospecting
- Develop a truly aggressive and consistent prospecting plan
- **Leverage** social sites to communicate, share, and grow sphere of influence exponentially

### Introduction (Slide 2 of 2)
**Three keys to prospecting success:**
1. **Number of contacts** — volume matters
2. **Consistency of contacts** — regular outreach, not just when you need business
3. **System for tracking contacts** — CRM or method to follow up

### Your Database (Slide 1 of 2)
- **Information management** is key to all three prospecting success factors
- Better data management = more success
- Contact management software/mobile apps = quick access from any device
- More automated prospecting = more people reached consistently, less time/effort
- **CRM** tracks contacts, reminds when to call/email/text, automates some tasks
- Start with **five categories**, create a separate database for each

### Your Database (Slide 2 of 2)
**Five prospecting categories (database groups):**
1. **Sphere of Influence** — people you already know
2. **Expired Listings** — listings that didn't sell with previous agent
3. **For Sale By Owner (FSBO)** — owners selling without an agent
4. **Geographic Farm** — specific neighborhood you target consistently
5. **Past Clients** — repeat business and referrals

### Sphere of Influence (expanded)
- Start here FIRST — compile a list of ALL people you know and have interacted with
- Includes: friends, family, hair stylist, mechanic, doctor, lawyer, accountant, server, bartender, civic orgs, clubs, sporting events, school acquaintances — literally everyone
- Tip: input **10 people per day** until full list is entered
- List grows over time — add new people as you meet them

### Sphere of Influence — Prospecting Technique
- Tell people you're always looking for new business
- Ask permission to add them to your prospecting list
- Frame it as: "I'm growing my business and could use your help"
- Most people **love to help someone they admire and trust**
- Key: make sure your SOI regards you with trust and admiration

### Expired Listings (Slide 1 of 1)
- Properties placed on market but **failed to sell**
- Advantage: you already know asking price, commission, and that they want to sell
- Sellers are **extremely motivated** — property sat for months
- **Check MLS daily** for expired listings in your area
- Enter into your expired listings database
- Can **automate through MLS home page**

### For Sale By Owner — FSBO (Slide 1 of 1)
- Sellers already want to sell — convince them you're the one to do it
- **How to find FSBOs:**
  - Drive market area for "For Sale by Owner" signs
  - Check RE classifieds in local newspaper each Sunday
  - Any ad without a brokerage company name = FSBO
- Gather info by **checking the tax rolls**
- Enter into FSBO database

### Geographic Farm (Slide 1 of 1)
- **Geographic farm** = specific location/subdivision/area you concentrate on
- Goal: become THE expert — when sellers think RE, they think of you
- **Best farms** are homogeneous: similar prices, age, family type, recreation
- Choose area you're comfortable with and have special interest in
- Enter all home info into geographic farm database
- Automate using **MLS or county tax appraiser's tax rolls**

### Past Clients (Slide 1 of 1)
- **Best leads** — existing relationship, "free business" from past work
- After each transaction: enter seller/buyer info + **forwarding address**
- Database grows over time
- **Contact at least TWICE a year**: postcard, purchase anniversary, birthday card, calendar
- All communications must include: **company name, your name, photo, contact info**

### Prospecting for Sellers — Geographic Farm (Slide 1 of 4)
- **Long-term commitment — years, not months**
- Research before selecting: number of homes, price ranges, turnover rates, price trends, competition from other agents
- Must yield enough transactions to make a living + fund farming
- Start with **no more than 200 homes**, expand as business grows
- Ideally **close to where you work or live** — convenience = better service

### Prospecting for Sellers — Geographic Farm (Slide 2 of 4)
- Use MLS: search **sold properties within last year**
- Analyze: price range, volume of sales, which brokerages had listings
- **Spotting competition:**
  - One brokerage code dominant = someone is farming it
  - Many scattered brokers = likely **no one farming it** (opportunity)

### Prospecting for Sellers — Geographic Farm (Slide 3 of 4)

- **10-15% turnover = good farm** — less may not be worth it
- Example: 93 sold ÷ 700 homes = **13% turnover** (good)

- Establish via: direct mail, phone calls, emails, area newsletter — combination works best

## Chapter 4, Section 4: Listing Contracts
Subsections: (1) Introduction, (2) Types of Listing Contracts, (3) Preparing Contracts, (4) How to Complete the Exclusive Right of Sale Listing Agreement

### Introduction (Slide 1 of 2)
- **Listing contract** = employment agreement between your **broker and a property owner**
- Establishes: asking price, term of listing, obligations of owner, obligations of broker
- Must state: commission amount + condition of employment for earning that commission

### Introduction (Slide 2 of 2)
- In Florida, listing agreement can be **oral, written, or implied** — but written is best protection
- Written listing contract **must contain**:
  1. Definite termination date of the agreement
  2. Complete legal description of the property
  3. Price and terms offered
  4. Promise of compensation and specifics of that compensation
  5. Signatures of all property owners and the broker or licensee
- **NO automatic renewal clause allowed**
- Must provide owner with a **complete copy within 24 hours** of signing

### Types of Listing Contracts (Slide 1 of 1 — 4 sub-topics)
Four types:
1. Open listing
2. Exclusive agency or exclusive listing
3. Exclusive right of sale listing
4. Net listing

#### Open Listing
- Owner agrees to pay commission to **any broker who provides a buyer** at acceptable price/terms
- **One-sided (unilateral) contract** — only binds owner to pay upon broker performance
- Broker has NO obligation to perform
- Owner can give open listing to **any number of brokers**
- **Cannot be placed in the MLS**

#### Exclusive Agency or Exclusive Listing
- Owner can list with **only ONE broker** — **bilateral contract** (binding on both broker and owner)
- All other brokers must work through the listing broker
- Owner pays commission if broker performs under the listing contract terms
- **Owner reserves the right to sell the property themselves** — no commission owed in that case
- **CAN be placed in the MLS**

#### Exclusive Right of Sale Listing
- **Must be in writing and signed** — bilateral contract
- Owner lists with **only one broker**
- Owner agrees to pay listing broker **REGARDLESS of who actually finds the buyer**
- Owner owes commission even if they find the buyer themselves or sell through another broker during the term
- **Broker is BEST PROTECTED by this type** of listing agreement
- **CAN be placed in the MLS**

#### Net Listing (Slide 1 of 2)
- **Not technically a type of listing agreement** — any of the 3 types can be structured as a net listing
- Owner sets a **minimum amount** they want from the sale
- Broker's commission = **any amount above the set minimum** (capped at agreed commission amount)

#### Net Listing (Slide 2 of 2)
- **Example:** Patrick wants $225K from sale. Alice (broker) sells for $275K. Alice gets $50K commission. BUT if max commission in contract is $30K, Alice must give excess $20K to Patrick. Can't exceed agreed commission.
- **Illegal in many states** — gives unscrupulous brokers opportunity to take advantage of uninformed sellers
- **LEGAL in Florida but generally discouraged**
- Creates **conflict of interest** for the broker — violates duty to put client's interests above your own

### Preparing Contracts (Slide 1)
- Brokers/licensees can prepare: RE contracts, listing agreements, basic option contracts, buyer broker agreements
- **Know the contract** — you're asking clients to sign it; how can they trust you to negotiate if you don't understand what you're asking them to sign?
- Brokers may only prepare leases **approved by the Florida Supreme Court**
- Using prepared lease form: licensee may only **fill in the blanks** — may NOT delete or add anything
- **Lease cannot be for more than one year**

### Preparing Contracts (Slide 2 of 3)
- Broker **CANNOT prepare**: deeds, mortgages, assignments, assumptions, or leases not approved by FL Supreme Court
- Refer clients to **attorney** for complicated option contracts or purchase/sales contracts
- **Exclusive Right of Sale Listing Agreement** (Form ERS-18tb Rev 5/2020) — approved by Florida Association of REALTORS
- Fill out the listing agreement **completely and correctly at the time of listing** — ensures seller complies and you get compensated

### Preparing Contracts (Slide 3 of 3)
- Refer to listing contract as an **"agreement"** when talking to seller — "would you mind signing our agreement"
- **Never leave a space blank** — if something doesn't apply, enter a zero or N/A

# CHAPTER 5: FINANCING REAL ESTATE

## Section 1: Mortgage Market and Selling Loans (62 slides)

### Lenders in the Mortgage Market

**Institutional vs. Noninstitutional Lenders:**
- **Institutional lender** = any financial institution whose loans and lending practices are regulated by law. They pool depositors' funds and invest in RE loans (financial intermediaries). Includes: commercial banks, savings associations, life insurance companies, pension funds.
- **Noninstitutional lender** = entity whose RE loans are NOT strictly regulated by state/federal agencies. Examples: private parties, syndications, private loan companies, endowment funds.

**Key Differences:**
1. **Regulation** — Institutional: highly regulated. Noninstitutional: few/no regulations.
2. **Direct vs. Intermediary** — Institutional: financial intermediaries. Noninstitutional: invest directly.
3. **Usury laws** — Institutional: NOT subject to usury laws (can charge any rate). Noninstitutional: MAY be subject to usury laws.
4. **Government loans** — Many institutional lenders can make VA/FHA loans; most noninstitutional cannot.
5. **Secondary market access** — Institutional: YES. Noninstitutional: NO.
6. **Rate lock-in** — Institutional: can offer 2-8 week lock-in. Noninstitutional: typically cannot.

### The Primary Mortgage Market

**Primary mortgage market** = where borrowers and mortgage originators come together to negotiate terms and effect mortgage transactions.

**Loan origination** = process from application through disbursal of funds (or declining).

**Sources in the Primary Mortgage Market:**

#### Commercial Banks
- Designed as depository + lender for commercial activities. Primarily short-term loans to businesses.
- Stock companies owned by stockholders.
- Demand accounts (checking) = highly fluid, rarely used for mortgages.
- Time deposits (savings) = less liquid, used for long-term investments including RE loans.
- Chartered by state (FL Office of Financial Regulation) or federal (OCC).
- All FL banks must have FDIC insurance.
- **Biggest RE impact: short-term construction loans** (3-12 months), home improvement loans (up to 5 yrs), manufactured housing loans (~10 yrs).
- Can make home loans up to 95% LTV, 30-year term. Require PMI on loans > 80% LTV.

#### Savings Associations (Thrifts/Savings Banks)
- Originally organized to help members finance residential property. Members pooled money, took turns building homes.
- Dominated residential mortgage market until 1980s deregulation.
- After deregulation: bad loans, poor decisions, fraud → **FIRREA (1989)** passed to reorganize.
- **RTC (Resolution Trust Corporation)** formed to manage insolvent thrifts, sell assets. Closed Dec 1996.
- Today: organized as stock (stockholder-owned) or mutual (depositor-owned).
- Offer savings accounts (time deposits) and NOW accounts (demand/checking equivalent).
- Must hold minimum % of assets as mortgage-related loans to borrow from Federal Home Loan Bank System.
- Often offer higher interest rates on deposits than commercial banks.
- Chartered by federal govt or by states. State-chartered can choose OCC/SAIF regulation or state regulation.

#### Life Insurance Companies
- Hold a major portion of American public's savings. Only savings associations control more.
- **Largest source of funds for apartment projects and commercial properties.**
- Major source for: shopping centers, office buildings, hotels/motels, industrial buildings, large apartment complexes.
- Typically invest up to 1/3 of assets in RE loans.
- Organized as: mutual companies (owned by policyholders) or stock companies (owned by stockholders).
- Licensed by home state. In FL: regulated by FL Office of Insurance Regulation.
- Prefer larger RE projects (safety/stability). Recently more active in single-family via mortgage bankers/brokers.

#### Private Parties (Individuals)
- Noninstitutional, very few regulations. Practices vary greatly.
- **Florida Usury Laws (F.S. 687.02):**
  - Loans up to $500,000: max 18% interest
  - Loans above $500,000: max 25% interest
  - 25%-45%: misdemeanor (2nd degree) — up to 60 days imprisonment
  - Above 45%: **loan sharking** — 3rd degree felony, up to 5 years imprisonment
- If found guilty of usury, borrower may not have to pay ANY interest.

#### REITs (Real Estate Investment Trusts)
- Created 1960 by federal tax law. Goal: small investors combine resources for RE ventures.
- Operates like a mutual stock fund. Investments primarily in apartment complexes and commercial properties.
- **Types:** Equity trusts (buy/own/manage), Mortgage trusts (lend money), Mixed trusts (both).
- **Tax benefits:** Exempt from corporate tax IF: invest ≥75% of assets in RE AND distribute ≥95% of annual RE income to investors.
- Target: income-producing properties, diversified by type and geography.

#### Syndications
- Group of 2+ people pooling resources for an investment.
- NOT a form of legal ownership — describes multiple ownership of an investment.
- Can operate as: REIT, corporation, general partnership, limited partnership, or tenancy in common.

#### Private Loan Companies
- Deal mostly in **junior financing** (2nd deeds of trust) — borrowers pull equity for other purchases.
- Can make loans from own/borrowed funds, act as brokers, buy/sell junior instruments.
- Charge more interest and higher loan placement fees (offset junior loan risk).

#### Pension Funds
- Collect contributions from workers/employers, invest to create retirement money pool.
- Traditionally invested in stocks and bonds.
- Now purchasing existing RE loans in secondary market via mortgage-backed securities.
- Some funds developing programs for contributors to use pension funds to purchase homes.

#### Endowment Funds
- Transfer of money/property donated to an institution, principal kept intact.
- Invested for long-term compound interest impact. Fund managers want safe, high-income, long-term investments.
- Good source of mortgage financing for commercial and industrial properties.
- Many commercial banks and mortgage companies handle investments for endowment funds.

#### Credit Unions
- Nonprofit financial institutions. Members deposit via direct deposit.
- Pay no income tax → higher interest on deposits, lower rates on loans.
- Mostly short-term loans. RE loans tend to be 2nd mortgages or home improvement.
- Under Federal Credit Union Act: authority to make 30-year loans for principal residence.
- Can make FHA/VA loans at comparable market rates.

#### Mortgage Loan Originators
- **DO NOT make loans.** Arrange loans by taking applications and searching for best rates.
- Charge application fees, earn finder's fee/commission.
- Must obtain license from FL Dept of Financial Services (DFS).
- Do NOT service the loan after placement.
- Must qualify borrowers and examine investment reliability.

#### Mortgage Bankers
- Financial intermediaries arranging flow of money from investors to borrowers. DO NOT have depositors.
- Originate mortgages → keep in portfolio or sell to investors.
- **Warehousing** = borrow from commercial bank using owned mortgages as collateral → make more loans → sell loans → repay bank loan.
- Often act as loan correspondents for insurance companies/pension funds.
- Usually continue to **service loans** (collect payments, escrow taxes/insurance, keep records, forward proceeds).
- Charge servicing fee + loan origination fees + points.
- **Largest originators of FHA and VA loans.**

### Selling and Packaging of Loans

Three ways mortgage funds can be sold/packaged:
1. **Direct sale** — Lender sells loans to another lender (e.g., FL S&L sells to SD S&L to balance supply/demand).
2. **Mortgage participation** — Sell partial interest in a block of loans (e.g., sell 90%, retain 10%, continue servicing).
3. **Mortgage-backed securities (MBS)** — Loans pooled together, securities issued representing claims on principal + interest payments.

### The Secondary Mortgage Market

- Developed to provide constant source of funds for RE loans.
- Secondary lenders purchase mortgages originated in primary market.
- Provides **liquidity** to primary market — when loans are sold, original funds are replaced for new loans.
- Primary lender profits from origination fees and points; usually continues servicing.
- Consists of holding warehouse agencies that package loans for resale to investors.
- **Discounts** used to adjust yields for buyer/seller negotiation.
- **Estoppel letter** confirms loan balance before sale in secondary market.
- Federal law requires borrower notification when loan is sold. Original terms unaffected.

**Principal Secondary Lenders (Government-Sponsored Enterprises/GSEs):**

#### Ginnie Mae (GNMA)
- Government-owned corporation within HUD.
- Created 1968. Mission: create/operate MBS program for **FHA and VA mortgages**.
- Developed first mortgage-backed security in 1970.
- **Pass-through security** = monthly principal + interest collected from borrowers, passed through to investors.

#### Fannie Mae (FNMA)
- Originally created 1938 as government corporation to purchase FHA loans.
- 1944: authorized to purchase VA loans.
- **1968 Charter Act**: became for-profit, private stockholder-owned corporation. Subject to federal corporate tax but exempt from state income taxes. HUD oversight continued.
- 1970: expanded to purchase conventional loans.
- **Oldest and largest participant in secondary mortgage market.**
- Loans meeting Fannie Mae/Freddie Mac standards = **conforming loans** (lenders = conforming lenders).
- Non-conforming loans must be held as **portfolio loans**.
- **Desktop underwriting** = lender inputs application by computer directly to secondary market for near-instant approval.
- Shareholder-owned company. Does NOT lend directly to homebuyers. Ensures lenders don't run out of mortgage funds.
- Stock traded on NYSE, part of S&P 500.

#### Freddie Mac (FHLMC)
- Introduced first security backed by conventional loans.
- **Stockholder-owned, for-profit corporation** operating as function of HUD.
- Authorized to purchase all loan types but primarily active in **conventional loans**.
- Sells MBS and mortgage loans to investors.

### Federal Laws Affecting RE Practice
- Chapters 455 and 475 of Florida Statutes + FREC rules (61J2 FAC) govern RE practice.
- NAR Code of Ethics for members.
- Federal environmental laws: National Environmental Policy Act, Clean Air Act, Solid Waste Disposal Act, Federal Water Pollution Act.
- **FIRPTA**: Sale of residential property > $300,000 owned by foreign investor. Buyers withhold 10% ($300K-$1M) or 15% (>$1M).

## Section 2: Loan Features (35 slides)

### Loan Fees
- **Loan origination fee** — typically 1% of loan amount, covers lender's cost for generating the loan.
- **Points (discount points)** — prepaid interest. Each point = 1% of loan amount. Paid at closing.
  - Example: 2 points on $150,000 = $3,000
- **Effective yield** — actual rate received. Rule of thumb: each point = 1/8% (0.125%) increase.
  - Example: 3 points × 0.125 = 0.375. So 6.25% + 0.375 = 6.625%

### Loan-to-Value Ratios (LTV)
- LTV = mortgage principal ÷ property value
- Example: $240K home, $48K down, $192K loan = 80% LTV
- Higher LTV = larger loan, smaller down payment
- Lenders use LTV to determine max loan amount. Lower LTV = less risk of default.

### Repayment Period (Loan Term)
- Most common: 30 years, 15 years, 20 years
- Longer term = lower monthly payment BUT more total interest
  - $150K @ 6% for 30 yrs: $899.33/mo, $173,757 total interest
  - $150K @ 6% for 15 yrs: $1,265.79/mo, $77,841 total interest

### Amortization
- **Fully amortized loan** — same payment every month. Payment goes first to interest, then principal.
- Early years: mostly interest, little principal. Later years: mostly principal.
  - $150K, 30yr, 6%: first payment only $149.33 to principal. By year 21: $524.79 to principal.

### Loan Repayment Plans

#### Fully Amortized, Fixed-Rate Loan
- Payment stays same over life of loan. Interest portion decreases, principal portion increases each month.
- **Interest + Principal = Monthly mortgage payment**
- 15-year fixed: lower interest rate (less risk), saves substantial interest vs 30-year.

#### Partially Amortized with Balloon Payment
- Payment not sufficient to fully pay off loan by end of term.
- **Balloon payment** = remaining unpaid balance due at end.
- Example: $125K @ 5%, amortized on 30-yr schedule but 20-yr term. Payment: $671.03/mo. Balloon at year 20: $63,527.64.

#### Interest-Only Loan
- Payments = interest only. No principal paid. Balloon payment of entire principal at end.
- Good for: short-term ownership expecting appreciation, investors wanting cash flow.
- Risky if property doesn't appreciate. Good for first-time buyers deferring large payments.

#### Adjustable Rate Mortgage (ARM)
- Interest rate fluctuates over loan term. Reduces lender's risk.
- **Index** — foundation rate, published, beyond lender's control. Common: one-year T-bill rate, 11th district cost of funds.
- **Margin** — percentage added to index by lender. Does NOT change for life of loan.
- **Note rate = Index + Margin**
- **Rate adjustment date** = when rate can change. **Rate adjustment period** = time between changes.
- 12 months = one-year ARM, 36 months = three-year ARM, 60 months = five-year ARM.
- **Negative amortization** — when payment is less than interest due, unpaid interest added to principal.
- **Payment cap** — limit on any single payment adjustment. **Lifetime cap** — max rate increase over life of loan.
  - Example: 2/6 caps = max 2% change per year, max 6% total over life.

#### Graduated Payment Mortgage (GPM)
- Low initial payments, periodically increase over first 3-5 years, then level off.
- Initial payments may be insufficient → **negative amortization** (borrower owes more than originally borrowed).

### Appraisal Principles (from Section 2 slides)
- **Principle of Anticipation** — value today = sum of future benefits.

## Section 3: Types of Mortgages (81 slides)

Only THREE types of mortgages:
1. **Conventional** — not insured/guaranteed by government
2. **FHA insured** — insured by FHA/HUD
3. **VA guaranteed** — guaranteed by VA

### Conventional Loans
- Any loan NOT insured or guaranteed by a government agency.
- Predominant method for financing single-family residences.
- Higher down payment, higher interest rate, more difficult to qualify than FHA/VA.
- Higher risk in foreclosure (no insurance/guarantee).
- Common: fixed-rate 30-year. 15-year increasingly popular (interest savings).

#### Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI)
- Introduced by MGIC in 1957. Required when LTV > 80%.
- With PMI: can get up to 95% LTV conventional loan.
- **HPA (1998)** — requires automatic cancellation of PMI when LTV reaches 78% of original value.
- PMI increases cost (borrower pays premium), but interest only on unpaid balance.

#### Qualifying Ratios (Conventional)
- **Front-end (housing expense ratio)** = PITI ÷ gross monthly income. Max: **28%**
- **Back-end (total obligations ratio)** = (PITI + other debts) ÷ gross monthly income. Max: **36%**
- Some lenders require 20% down and 720+ credit score.

#### Amortizing Loan Payments
- Formula: **I = P × R × T** (Interest = Principal × Rate × Time)
- Example: $100K, 30yr, 4%. Payment: $477.42. Month 1: $333.33 interest, $144.09 principal.

#### Conventional Loan Options
- **Biweekly mortgage** — half payment every 2 weeks (26 payments/yr = 13 monthly). Pays off ~25 yrs instead of 30. Saves substantial interest.
- **Graduated Payment Mortgage (GPM)** — payments increase yearly for 3-5 years, then level off. May cause negative amortization.
- **Pledged Account Mortgage (PAM)** — down payment put in pledged account, draws supplement payments for first 3-5 years.
- **Buydown** — lump sum (usually from builder/family) paid at closing to reduce rate for first few years.
- **Open-end loan** — expandable loan up to a limit. Fixed rate on original, prevailing rate on advances.
- **Blanket loan** — covers multiple properties. Used by developers. **Partial release clause** allows releasing individual lots.
- **Wraparound loan** — new larger loan wraps existing loan. Second lender pays first. Only possible if original docs allow.
- **Bridge loan** — short-term between end of one loan and start of another.
- **Purchase Money Mortgage (PMM)** — buyer borrows from seller as second mortgage.
- **Land contract (Contract for Deed)** — buyer gets equitable title only; seller keeps legal title until fully paid.
- **Construction loan** — disbursed in stages as construction completes. Higher rates, short-term. **Takeout loan** = permanent loan that pays off construction loan.
- **Home equity loan** — borrow against equity. Fixed amount or line of credit.
- **Package mortgage** — includes personal property/appliances (common in furnished condos).
- **Reverse Annuity Mortgage (RAM)** — lender makes payments TO borrower. For older homeowners. Repaid when home sells or from estate.
- **Sale and leaseback** — owner sells property, leases it back. Used by commercial enterprises for working capital.

### FHA Loans
- FHA does NOT build homes or loan money directly. **Insures loans** made by approved institutions.
- Protects lenders against loss from borrower default.

#### FHA Loan Facts
- **Term:** 10-, 15-, 20-, 25-, or 30-year fixed; or 30-year adjustable.
- **Down payment:** Min 3.5% with FICO ≥ 580. 10% if FICO 500-579. Below 500: not eligible.
- Down payment can include non-repayable gifts (need gift letter).
- **Max LTV:** 96.5%
- **Closing costs** can be rolled in (loan + closing ≤ 96.5% of lesser of appraised value or selling price).
- **No prepayment penalty** on 1-4 family. 30-day written notice required for early payoff.
- **Max loan fee:** 1% of loan amount (paid by buyer or seller).
- **Mortgage Insurance Premiums (MIP):**
  - **UFMIP (Up-front):** 1.75% of mortgage amount, paid at closing (can be financed).
  - **AMIP (Annual):** varies by LTV/term/amount. If LTV ≤ 90% at origination: drops off after 11 years. If LTV > 90%: AMIP for life of loan.
- **Default:** FHA insures lender **100%** — reimbursed for losses including foreclosure costs.
- **Interest rate:** negotiated between lender and borrower (market-driven).
- **Escrow account:** required for taxes, insurance, MIP.
- **No due-on-sale clause.** Original terms stay same.
- **Assumable** (rules vary by date/type/program). Only lender (not FHA) can release original borrower.
- **Appraisal:** must use approved FHA appraiser. Conditional commitment valid 6 months (existing) / 1 year (new construction).
- **No max purchase price.** Buyer can pay more than appraisal but loan based on appraisal.
- **Owner-occupied** required. Must meet FHA property/construction/neighborhood standards.
- **Pest inspection** required.

#### FHA Qualifying Ratios
- **Front-end:** max **31%** (PITI ÷ gross monthly income)
- **Back-end:** max **43%** (total obligations ÷ gross monthly income)
- Back-end includes: auto loans, student loans, personal loans, charge cards (minimums), child support, alimony, federal tax liens — EXCEPT items with < 9 months remaining.
- NOT included: cell phone, utilities, health/car insurance, anything not on credit report.

#### FHA Loan Programs
- **Section 203(b)** — basic mortgage insurance for purchase/refinance of owner-occupied 1-4 family.
- **Section 203(k)** — rehabilitation mortgage insurance.
- **Section 234(c)** — condominiums (30-year, single unit).
- **Section 251** — ARMs. 1/3/5-year: max 1%/yr, 5% lifetime. 7/10-year: max 2%/yr, 6% lifetime.

#### FHA Loan Limits
- Set by county. FL range: $271,050 (Ocala) to $729,750 (Key West/Monroe).

### VA Loans
- Little or no down payment. Easy qualification. Low interest rates.
- **Funding fee** — percentage of loan amount, varies by loan type and military category. Can be financed.
- **Certificate of eligibility** — from VA, shows guarantee amount.
- **Entitlement** — max $106,025. Can be reused if prior VA loan repaid or assumed by another veteran.
- **Eligibility:** Peacetime: 181 days. Wartime/conflict: 90 days. Service-related disability: automatic. Currently 90 days (Gulf War period never ended).
- Surviving spouse eligible if veteran KIA, died from service injuries, MIA, or POW.
- **Interest rate:** set by lender (negotiated), NOT by VA.
- **Points:** set by lender. Each point = 1% of loan. Paid by buyer or seller per contract.

#### VA Appraisals
- Approved VA appraiser issues **Certificate of Reasonable Value (CRV)**.
- Loan cannot exceed CRV. Valid 6 months (existing) / 12 months (new construction).
- CRV will NEVER exceed sales price.
- If sales price > CRV: veteran pays difference in cash (source must be VA-approved) or can withdraw.

#### VA Uses
- Buy home, townhouse, condo (VA-approved), build, repair/alter/improve, refinance, buy manufactured home/lot, energy improvements.

## Section 4: Seller Financing and Lending (26 slides)

### Seller Financing
- When seller helps buyer finance the purchase.

#### Advantages
- Makes property more marketable (especially with high rates/tight lending).
- Helps buyers who can't qualify for lender financing. Avoids points/loan fees. Lower down payment possible.
- Seller may get higher price (buyers pay more for favorable terms).
- **Tax benefits** for seller — report profit only as received each year.

#### Types of Seller Financing
- **Primary seller financing** — seller is ONLY source of buyer's financing.
- **Purchase money loan** — buyer signs promissory note + mortgage/deed of trust to seller. Installment payments over time.
- **Installment land sales contract (Contract for Deed/Land Contract)** — buyer gets equitable title only. Seller keeps legal title until fully paid. Buyer pays down payment + regular P&I. Buyer pays taxes, insurance, maintenance. Many contracts allow seller to cancel + keep all payments on default.
- **Buydown** — seller reduces net proceeds at closing to fund rate reduction for buyer's first few years.
- **Closing costs** — seller pays some closing costs (within lender limits) so buyer needs less cash.
- **Lease option** — tenant gets right to purchase at predetermined price within set period. Rent credit toward purchase price is optional.

### Truth in Lending (TILA / Regulation Z)
- Federal law requiring lenders to disclose true cost of credit.
- **Applies to:** all loans secured by a residence.
- **Does NOT apply to:** commercial loans or agricultural loans over $25,000.

#### Key Provisions
1. **Disclosure** — lender must disclose all finance charges as **APR** (annual percentage rate). Also **TIP** (total interest percentage — sum of all interest over life as % of original loan).
2. **Right to rescind** — 3 days to cancel. Does NOT apply to residential first mortgages. DOES apply to refinancing and home equity loans.
3. **Advertising "triggering terms"** — if ad includes: down payment amount/%, installment amount, finance charge, number of installments, repayment period, or "no charge for credit" → must disclose ALL of: down payment, repayment terms, APR (and if increase possible), total finance charge, total payments and due dates.
4. **Penalties** — violation: 2× finance charge (min $100, max $1,000) + court costs + attorney fees + actual damages. **Willful violation:** misdemeanor, up to $5,000 fine or 1 year prison or both.

## Section 5: The Loan Application Process (38 slides)

### Pre-qualifying Your Buyer
- Have buyer meet lender BEFORE house hunting. Determines affordable price range.
- Buyer's responsibility to start mortgage process per contract timeline.
- **Three requirements to qualify:**
  1. **Assets** — enough for down payment + closing costs + prepaids (not just down payment!). Can use gifts from friends/family.
  2. **Credit score** — bad credit can be fixed. Professionals can help improve scores.
  3. **Income** — lender uses **AGI (Adjusted Gross Income)**, NOT gross income. AGI = gross minus adjustments (educator expenses, student loan interest, alimony, business expenses, retirement contributions).

### The Mortgage Process
- **Mortgage underwriting** = risk evaluation process.
- Loan processor takes application → mortgage underwriter reviews/verifies.
- Underwriter contacts employers, past employers, creditors.
- Primary risk: borrower default. If default → foreclosure, but no guarantee proceeds cover loan balance.

### The Loan Application — Required Documents
- Purchase contract, bank account info, 2-3 months checking/savings statements
- 2 years tax returns, recent pay stubs, W-2s
- Credit card bills, other debt info (car, student loans, etc.)
- Self-employed: balance sheets + tax returns
- Gift money: **gift letter** required
- Application fee and appraisal fee due at application

### Qualifying the Borrower

#### Income
- Must be sufficient and **reasonably dependable/stable**.
- 2+ years continuous employment in same field = general criteria.
- Acceptable: wages, bonuses, commissions, overtime, part-time, self-employment, retirement, alimony/child support, investment income.
- NOT acceptable: temporary employment, unemployment compensation, family contributions.
- Verification: employer contact, Request for Verification of Employment form, W-2s, pay stubs.
- Self-employed: 2 years tax returns, audited financial statements.
- Alimony/child support: court orders + bank statements showing receipt.

#### Debt
- **Income ratio (front-end)** = housing costs (PITI + assessments) ÷ gross income
- **Debt ratio (back-end)** = (housing + other monthly obligations) ÷ gross income
- Each loan program (conventional/FHA/VA) has own ratio requirements.

#### Net Worth
- Assets minus liabilities. Important if income quality is borderline.
- Cash-heavy net worth = encouraging to lender.
- Some lenders require cash reserves (few months of mortgage payments) after purchase.

#### Credit History
- Credit report covers preceding **7 years**.
- Negatives: slow repayment, collections, repos, foreclosures, judgments, bankruptcies.
- **FICO scores:** 300-850. Higher = lower risk.
- **Payment history = most important factor (35%)**
- FICO breakdown: Payment history 35%, Outstanding debt 30%, Credit inquiries 15%, Credit history 10%, Type of credit 10%
- Below 580 = poor. Above 660 = excellent.
- Below 580: not auto-denied but higher rate and limited options.

### Qualifying the Property
- Appraiser evaluates property, issues market value estimate.
- Loan based on **lesser of appraisal or sales price**.

## Section 6: Mortgage Fraud (12 slides)

### Two Categories
1. **Fraud for property** — applicant misrepresents to purchase (embellishes income, hides debt). Usually single loan. Intent to repay.
2. **Fraud for profit** — multiple loans, elaborate schemes for illicit proceeds. Gross misrepresentations. Participants often paid.

### Types of Mortgage Fraud (FBI)
- **Property flipping** — purchase, falsely appraise higher, quickly sell. Fraudulent appraisal = illegal.
- **Nominee loans / straw buyers** — concealed identity, nominee's name and credit used.
- **Fictitious/stolen identity** — fake or stolen identity on application.
- **Silent second** — buyer borrows down payment from seller via undisclosed second mortgage. Not recorded.
- **Inflated appraisals** — appraiser in collusion provides false high value.
- **Equity skimming** — stolen identity to get mortgage, collect rent, never pay, let foreclose.
- **Employment fraud** — lies about employment/position/company to justify false income.
- **Occupancy fraud** — claims primary residence but purchasing as investment (gets better rate).
- **Shotgunning** — multiple loans on same property simultaneously, obtaining funds above property value.

### Foreclosure Schemes
- Target people at risk of losing home. Charge up-front fee, promise to save home, then disappear.
- **Foreclosure rescue** — purchase property, lease back to owner. May or may not allow repurchase (often at excessive price/rate).
- **Fake refinance** — homeowner thinks they're refinancing but actually signs docs selling the home → faces eviction.

## Section 2: Preparing the Buyer (30 slides)
**Key Topics:** Qualifying buyers, Interviewing buyers, Protecting your interest, Buyer's interview of you

- **Qualify buyers:** Your time is valuable. Work only with qualified buyers. Get name and number early — if they won't give it, not worth pursuing
- **Phone qualifying:** Keep them on phone to gauge motivation. Have computer ready for quick property search. Ask: areas interested in? Price range? How long looking? Working with another licensee?

## Section 4: Preparing for the Sale (38 slides)
**Key Topics:** Buyer agreements, Showing property, Making an offer, Buyer objections, Offer contract, Seller financing

- **Showing Agreement (FAR SA-3x):** List addresses of shown properties. If buyer purchases shown property through another broker, they owe you compensation per agreement. If they buy one you didn't show, they owe nothing
- **Exclusive Buyer Brokerage Agreement (FAR EBBA-6tn):** Exclusive representation, retainer fee, liability limitation, protection period after termination. More advantages than Showing Agreement
- **Showing property:** Don't just point out rooms. Present property at its best, highlight unique features/upgrades. Eliminate objections by watching reactions/expressions
- **Show best buy FIRST:** Buyer more motivated by fear of losing first house than gaining last. Limit to max 5 showings at a time
- **Scheduling:** Give listing agent window (20 min arrival). Be punctual. Let listing agent "sell" the property. After showing ask: "If this were the only one, would you buy it?"
- **Don't show to large families** — have children stay home until final selection
- **Stay with buyer** — liability issue (injury or missing items). Leave property as found
- **Disclosures:** All sellers/brokers must disclose material defects not readily visible. Written disclosure preferred for liability protection
- **No requirement to disclose:** Homicide/suicide/death (F.S. 689.25), HIV/AIDS status
- **Making an offer:** Watch for readiness signals — expressions softening, wanting to revisit, bringing measuring tools/contractor. Acknowledge objections, don't dismiss
- **Handle price objections:** Ask what they think is fair and how they arrived at it. Show CMA comparables. Warn that going too low may put off seller
- **Overcoming reluctant buyer:** Create urgency with facts — days on market, number of showings, property may not be there tomorrow
- **Offer contract:** Review every blank with buyer. They must understand it's binding if accepted. Seller will likely counter on 2-3 points only. Advise on pricing (CMA), earnest money, personal/real property, warranties, legal description, contingencies, closing date
- **Pre-qualification vs pre-approval:** Pre-qual = basic financial info, quick. Pre-approval = verified employment/income, stronger in multiple-offer situations
- **Contingencies:** More contingencies = less attractive offer. Common: financing, home inspection (5-10 days), sale of current home
- **Final walk-through:** STRONGLY advise. Bring contract copy. Verify inclusions and repairs. Have buyer sign off on acceptance
- **Seller financing:** Seller can help buyer finance. Types include purchase money mortgages

## Section 5: Contracts (84 slides — HEAVY on contract details)
**Key Topics:** Statute of Frauds, Essential elements, Purchase & Sale contracts, AS IS contracts, Option contracts, Contract negotiation, Contract termination

### Contract Fundamentals
- **Statute of Frauds (F.S. 725.01):** RE contracts must be in writing to be enforceable (sales, leases >1 year, options, deeds, mortgages). Oral contract not invalid/illegal — just unenforceable
- **Oral contract exceptions (need 2 of 3):** Partial payment, improvements to property, buyer possession
- **Statute of Limitations:** Written contracts = 5 years. Oral contracts = 4 years
- **Essential elements:** Lawful subject, Agreement (offer + acceptance = meeting of the minds), Consideration, Competent parties
- **Agreement:** Offeror makes offer → offeree receives. Any changes = counteroffer, original offer dies. Executed contract must be communicated quickly
- **Consideration:** Valuable (money, property, enforceable promise) or Good ("love and affection")
- **Competent parties:** Sane, sober, competent, age 18+. Under 18 = unenforceable. Mentally incompetent = could void

### Residential Contract for Sale and Purchase (37 detailed line-by-line slides)
- **Page 1:** Property description, legal description, personal property included/excluded, purchase price, deposits, escrow agent, time for acceptance, closing date
- **Page 2:** Occupancy/possession, assignability, financing (cash/contingent/assume/seller)
- **Page 3:** Closing costs (seller pays/buyer pays), title insurance, general repair caps, WDO treatment caps, building permit close-out costs
- **Page 4:** Title evidence (who designates closing agent — seller/buyer/Miami-Dade-Broward provision), home warranty (buyer/seller/N/A), special assessments
- **Page 5:** Disclosures, property maintenance (seller's responsibility), inspection period (enter # days)
- **Pages 6-7:** Property condition/inspections
- **Pages 7-8:** Escrow agent responsibilities, professional advice/broker liability
- **Page 8:** Default (buyer defaults → deposit split between listing/cooperating brokers; seller defaults → deposit returned, buyer may seek damages or specific performance)
- **Dispute Resolution:** 10 days after conflicting demands, then mediation (certified mediator with RE experience), then litigation
- **Attorney's Fees:** Split mediator's fee; litigation = prevailing party paid
- **Standards (18):** A-Title, B-Survey, C-Ingress/Egress, D-Lease Info, E-Liens, F-Time (calendar days), G-Force Majeure (14 days beyond closing = terminate), H-Conveyance, I-Closing Location, J-Escrow Closing, K-Prorations, L-Access for Appraisals/Inspections, M-Risk of Loss (>1.5% purchase price), N-1031 Exchange, O-Contract Not Recordable, P-Integration/Modification, Q-Waiver, R-Riders/Addenda control over printed provisions, S-Collection, T-RESERVED, U-Florida Law/Venue, V-FIRPTA

### "AS IS" Contract
- FAR BAR "AS IS" — used for distressed properties (foreclosures, short sales) and when seller won't pay repairs
- **Key differences from standard:** No seller repair obligations (general, WDO, building permits). Includes buyer's RIGHT TO CANCEL. Section 18(X) Buyer Waiver of Claims added
- Does NOT relieve seller/broker of disclosing material defects

### Option Contracts
- **Right** (not obligation) to buy during specified period at specified price. Unilateral contract
- **Parties:** Optionor (gives right) = obligated. Optionee (receives right) = has choice
- **Requirements:** In writing (Statute of Frauds), state price/terms, specified time period, legal description, definite valuable consideration
- **Exercising:** Optionee notifies optionor → becomes purchase and sale contract (bilateral, binding on both)
- **Option money:** Only partial refund if not exercised (full refund = no consideration = void). May be applied to purchase price if exercised. F.S. 475.43

### Contract Negotiation
- Present offer to seller ASAP. Listing agent usually presents. Go over entire offer with listing agent
- May accompany to presentation if buyer has special circumstances — must disclose agency status
- Seller can: Accept as-is, Reject totally, or Counteroffer
- **Counteroffer terminates original offer.** Counteroffer = new offer
- **Tips for counteroffers:** Don't pressure buyer, rewrite whole paragraphs when changing important terms, reference original clause, don't change just for the sake of change, date and sign properly

### Contract Termination
- **Breach:** Failure to perform. Injured party may seek legal action
- **Revocation:** Legally entitled party terminates
- **Mutual rescission:** Both parties agree to terminate
- **Lapse of time:** If no time stated, "reasonable time" (court decides)
- **Abandonment:** No movement toward performance
- **Performance:** All parties fulfill obligations → contract terminated (completed)
